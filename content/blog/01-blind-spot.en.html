Your OCR tools read an electricity bill flawlessly.

But what happens when a client sends you a blurry WhatsApp photo of a purchase receipt written in Mandarin, with no matching proof of payment attached?

That's where OCR stops. And the manual work begins.

OCR solved the standard. Not the exception.

Dext, Receipt Bank, Pennylane: these are excellent machines for reading standard, well-formed invoices. E-invoicing will automate that flow even further in the months ahead.

But those tools were never built to do one thing: chase down the missing document, in the right order, from the right client.

And that's exactly where your team burns its hours:

  • The incorporation certificate, the articles of association and bank details requested when opening a new file.
  • The partners' ID documents when a company is being formed.
  • The exception items: foreign purchase receipts (Asia, USA), mileage claims, unidentified bank transfers.
  • The bundled year-end or audit requests: loan agreements, stock statements as of December 31, miscellaneous attestations.

None of these documents arrives "on its own, neatly filed." Each one needs a follow-up, an explanation, a completeness check. And no OCR does that.

The difference: reading a document vs. guided collection

It's a simple distinction, but a structural one.

An OCR tool starts from a document you already have and extracts data from it.

A guided collection workflow starts from a need — "I need the registration certificate, the articles, and two sets of bank details for this file" — and walks the client through it until the file is complete and compliant.

These are opposite ends of the chain. One cleans up the output. The other secures the input.

As long as the input is still an email saying "could you re-send that?", the best OCR in the world will keep working on files full of holes.

What guided collection actually looks like

This is precisely the job of a pre-collection gateway, sitting upstream of your production software (Cegid, ACD, Ibiza, Pennylane). It doesn't replace it — it feeds it clean.

Concretely, with a tool like IntakeFlow:

  1. You build a journey per type of need (onboarding, year-end close, exception item), with multiple steps and a clear instruction for each requested document.
  2. The client gets a single link — no account to create, no password.
  3. Every upload is validated at the source: required documents present, accepted file format, file size. An unreadable or missing file is flagged before submission, not three follow-ups later.
  4. The complete file lands, structured, in an operator inbox where your team member processes it, assigns it, annotates it, then exports it to your document system.

It's not a competitor to your OCR. It's what should sit before it.

The real stake: what regulation doesn't cover

E-invoicing will industrialize the standard flow. Good: it frees up time on repetitive data entry.

But it doesn't touch client onboarding, exception items, or year-end supporting documents. That off-flow residue — the part that has always resisted automation — will mechanically become the most time-consuming part of your firm's production.

The firm that industrializes that collection will pull ahead on margin per file.

So the question isn't "which OCR should I choose." It's: who handles everything the OCR will never see?
