I spent months building a product I thought was solid.

Then I showed it to someone. They nodded politely and said: "Oh, so it's basically a form builder?"

And that's when I realized my real problem wasn't technical.

The code was the easy part

This will surprise people, but it's true: building the engine was the simplest part.

A multi-step journey, validated file uploads (format, size, presence of required documents), an operator inbox with statuses, assignments, internal notes, proof of payment, structured export, and even server-side rendering to embed it on WordPress without breaking the theme.

All of that is engineering. It's hard, but it's bounded. You know when it's done.

The real wall came after: saying clearly what it is.

"Form builder": the magnet for the wrong prospects

Every time I described the product as a tool to build forms, two things happened.

First, I'd be mentally filed next to Typeform, Google Forms, Gravity Forms — saturated categories where the price war is lost in advance against a free tool.

Second — and this was worse — I attracted the wrong people. Folks who wanted a quick contact form, tested it for five minutes, and left. Not operators who process files over and over and for whom the back office is worth its weight in gold.

The word "form" described the visible interface. It completely hid the real value: the private link, the guided portal, and the processing inbox. That triptych is the product. The form is just the surface.

It took me far too long to admit it, because "form builder" was easy to say. Clarity, on the other hand, requires giving up the easy thing.

The lesson: a horizontal product sells to no one

I'd built something horizontal — clubs, associations, local governments, agencies, regulated professions. Everyone could use it.

Which means, in practice: nobody saw themselves in it. When you speak to everyone, each listener hears "not really for me."

So the hardest decision wasn't adding a feature. It was removing one from the message: choosing a niche, and accepting not speaking to everyone else in order to truly speak to one audience.

Why accounting firms

I looked for the segment where the pain of the "off-flow" is sharpest, most quantifiable, and least well served.

Accounting firms ticked every box:

  • E-invoicing automates their standard flow, but leaves untouched onboarding (registration certificate, articles, bank details), exception items (foreign receipts, mileage claims), and year-end closes.
  • Their staff can spend up to 30% of their time chasing clients for documents — billable hours spent on zero value.
  • OCR tools like Dext or Pennylane don't do that. And nobody positions themselves clearly upstream, as a pre-collection gateway.

Everything I'd built — the no-login link, validation at the source, the inbox with assignment and internal notes — suddenly found one simple sentence: IntakeFlow is the pre-collection gateway for the documents your accounting tools don't know how to go and fetch.

What I'm taking away

Building means converting effort into features. It's tiring but linear.

Positioning means converting a product into a sentence a customer repeats to themselves. And that doesn't happen by coding. It happens by giving up — an easy category, an audience that's too broad, the urge to please everyone.

If I had to sum up the lesson in one line: your product doesn't exist until you can say, without hesitating, who it's for and what it replaces.
