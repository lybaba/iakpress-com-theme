The whole profession is talking about what e-invoicing is going to automate.

Almost nobody is talking about what it's going to leave untouched — and that's exactly where firms' margins will be decided in the coming years.

What the reform actually solves

Let's be fair: this is good news for firms.

The roll-out of mandatory e-invoicing (structured formats, certified platforms) will standardize and stabilize the flow of ordinary purchase and sales invoices. The format becomes normalized, the data arrives clean, and OCR and repetitive data entry shrink to almost nothing on that perimeter.

For standard data entry, that's real automation. The time saved is real.

What the reform doesn't touch (at all)

But a standardized invoice is only part of what enters an accounting file. Everything else — the off-flow — stays exactly as manual as before.

The reform doesn't solve:

  • Client onboarding. The registration certificate, the articles, bank details, partners' ID documents when opening a file. No invoicing platform brings you those.
  • Exception items. Foreign purchase receipts, mileage claims, proof for unidentified transfers, off-spec expense reports. Always a blurry photo, always by email.
  • Year-end closes and audits. The bundled end-of-period requests: loan agreements, stock statements as of December 31, attestations. E-invoicing has nothing to do with any of that.
  • Completeness tracking. Knowing, file by file, what has arrived and what's still missing.

This residue isn't a detail. It's precisely the part that already resisted automation. And as the standard gets automated, the off-flow mechanically becomes the most time-consuming part of your production.

The tipping point nobody is anticipating

Here's the reasoning few firms say out loud.

If the reform removes, say, half the time spent on standard data entry, it doesn't cut your workload in half. It changes the composition of your workload: the "complex documents, onboarding, year-end" share goes from a third of your time to the majority of it.

In other words: tomorrow's bottleneck isn't the invoice. It's everything around it.

The firm that industrializes that collection — not with one more OCR, but with a guided pre-collection gateway — will build a margin lead that's hard to catch.
